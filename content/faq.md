faq:
  - q: >-
      Is "natural supplements singapore" a regulated definition?
    a: >-
      No. In Singapore, as in most markets, "natural" is not a legally standardised term for supplement labelling. It describes ingredient origin — plant, mineral, or animal-sourced — but no regulatory body enforces a minimum standard a product must meet before using the word.
  - q: >-
      What does "clean label supplements singapore" usually mean?
    a: >-
      "Clean label" is a marketing phrase, not a certified standard. It generally describes a shorter ingredient list with fewer synthetic additives or fillers. Each manufacturer defines the term differently. The absence of a regulated definition means two products both calling themselves "clean label" may have very different formulations.
  - q: >-
      Are herbal supplements in Singapore always "natural"?
    a: >-
      Herbal supplements use plant-derived active ingredients, so they are generally described as natural. However, herbal does not mean safe for all individuals or free from interactions. Some botanical ingredients interact with prescribed medications, and concentrated extracts can behave very differently from the whole plant. Always check with a pharmacist or clinician if you are on any medication.
  - q: >-
      What should I check first on a natural supplement label?
    a: >-
      Start with the full ingredient list. Look for botanical or scientific names, disclosed quantities for each active ingredient, country of manufacture, and any third-party certification logos. Check what each certification actually verifies — contaminant testing is different from potency verification.
  - q: >-
      Are "additive-free supplements singapore" claims reliable?
    a: >-
      The term has no enforced legal definition for supplements. It usually means the product omits certain synthetic colours, sweeteners, or preservatives, but each manufacturer decides what counts as an additive. Some additives serve functional roles such as moisture prevention or binding. The relevant question is whether all ingredients are identified and whether any conflict with your health background.
  - q: >-
      What is the difference between organic vs natural supplements singapore?
    a: >-
      "Organic" refers to how an ingredient was grown — without synthetic pesticides or certain inputs — and is verified by a certifying body such as USDA Organic. "Natural" refers only to ingredient origin and requires no third-party audit. An organic ingredient is natural by definition, but a natural ingredient is not automatically organic. Neither label alone speaks to potency, bioavailability, or clinical relevance.
  - q: >-
      How do I evaluate online listings for natural health products in Singapore?
    a: >-
      Look for a complete, readable ingredient list with quantities. Check for stable product information, identified manufacturer details, and any certifications. Be cautious if a listing relies mainly on testimonials, before-and-after language, or urgency pricing. Strong marketing language combined with vague ingredient disclosure is a pattern worth pausing on.
  - q: >-
      What are common marketing terms that mislead?
    a: >-
      Terms to be cautious about include "chemical-free" (technically inaccurate — all matter is chemical), "100% natural" (unverified claim), "clinically proven" without a cited study, "detox" without a defined mechanism, and "boosts immunity" without qualification. None of these are regulated claims in the supplement category.
  - q: >-
      How do I compare ingredient transparency across brands?
    a: >-
      Look for brands that list each active ingredient by name and amount, name the specific chemical form (for example, magnesium citrate rather than just "magnesium"), disclose the source or country of origin, and provide third-party testing documentation. Proprietary blends that list ingredients without individual amounts make comparison difficult by design.
  - q: >-
      When should I pause and ask a clinician?
    a: >-
      Consult a clinician or pharmacist before starting a supplement if you are on prescribed medication, pregnant, breastfeeding, managing a diagnosed condition, or considering a product that claims to affect a specific health outcome. Bring the product label to your appointment so they can review the full ingredient list in context of your situation.
