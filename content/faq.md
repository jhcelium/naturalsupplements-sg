faq:
  - q: Is "natural" a regulated definition in supplements?
    a: In most markets, including Singapore, "natural" is not a legally standardised term for supplement labelling. It typically refers to ingredient origin — plant, mineral, or animal-sourced — but there is no single enforced definition a product must meet before using the word.
  - q: What should I check first on a "natural" supplement label?
    a: Start with the full ingredient list. Look for botanical or scientific names, country of origin, and any third-party certification logos. Check what each certification actually verifies — testing for contaminants is different from testing for potency or label accuracy.
  - q: Are "clean label" and "natural" the same?
    a: No. "Natural" describes ingredient origin. "Clean label" usually means a shorter ingredient list with fewer additives. A product can be one without being the other. Neither term has a legally enforced definition in most markets.
  - q: How do I evaluate online listings?
    a: Look for stable, detailed product information with a readable ingredient list and quantities. Check for clear seller contact details. Be cautious of listings that rely mainly on testimonials, vague promises, or time-limited pricing.
  - q: Should I avoid additives completely?
    a: Not necessarily. Some additives serve a functional purpose, such as preventing moisture or binding ingredients safely. The relevant question is whether the additive amount is disclosed and whether any ingredient conflicts with your health situation — which is best discussed with a clinician or pharmacist.
